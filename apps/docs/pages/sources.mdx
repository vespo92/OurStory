# Source Types

OurStory research agents utilize multiple source categories to build comprehensive historical understanding.

## Source Categories

### Academic Sources

Peer-reviewed research, university publications, and scholarly works:

- Historical journals
- Academic books
- Dissertation research
- University archives

**Reliability:** Generally high for methodology, may have institutional biases

### Declassified Documents

Government documents released through:

- Freedom of Information Act (FOIA)
- Mandatory declassification review
- Congressional releases
- Presidential records

**Reliability:** Primary sources, but may be redacted or selectively released

### Religious Texts

Historical religious documents and commentaries:

- Sacred texts (Bible, Torah, Quran, etc.)
- Theological interpretations
- Religious historical accounts
- Manuscript traditions

**Reliability:** Primary for religious perspectives, interpretation varies

### Alternative History

Non-mainstream historical analysis:

- Independent researchers
- Investigative journalists
- Whistleblower accounts
- Revisionist historians

**Reliability:** Variable, requires careful evaluation

### Archaeological Sources

Physical evidence and scientific analysis:

- Excavation reports
- Artifact analysis
- Dating studies
- Site surveys

**Reliability:** High for physical evidence, interpretation varies

### Oral Tradition

Passed-down accounts and cultural memory:

- Indigenous histories
- Family traditions
- Community memory
- Folklore with historical basis

**Reliability:** Valuable perspective, may lack precise dating

## Document Types

| Type | Description | Examples |
|------|-------------|----------|
| **Declassified** | Previously classified government documents | NSA intercepts, CIA reports |
| **Official** | Government statements and reports | Court inquiries, congressional testimony |
| **Leaked** | Unauthorized releases | Whistleblower documents |
| **Testimony** | Firsthand accounts | Survivor statements, depositions |
| **Academic** | Scholarly analysis | Peer-reviewed papers, books |

## Evaluating Sources

Each source is evaluated on:

1. **Provenance** - Origin and chain of custody
2. **Corroboration** - Independent verification
3. **Bias** - Potential motivations affecting accuracy
4. **Contemporaneity** - Proximity to events described
5. **Expertise** - Author/source qualifications
6. **Completeness** - Potential for selective presentation
